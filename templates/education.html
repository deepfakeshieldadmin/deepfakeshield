Learn How Authenticity Scoring Works

Deep Fake Shield is designed not just to produce a result, but also to help users understand why a piece of content may appear trustworthy, suspicious, edited, or synthetic.

What is EXIF Metadata?

EXIF metadata is hidden data stored inside many photos captured by cameras and smartphones. It can include camera model, date taken, settings, and sometimes software traces.

  • Camera make and model details
  • Capture date and time
  • Photo settings like exposure and ISO
  • Software history in some cases

What if EXIF is missing?

Missing EXIF does not automatically mean the media is fake. It may be removed during screenshots, social media sharing, editing, or compression pipelines.

  • Social media often strips metadata
  • Screenshots usually do not contain camera EXIF
  • AI-generated images often lack natural camera metadata
  • Edited media may replace original metadata with software traces

What does “edited” mean?

Edited media can still originate from a real source, but has been modified after capture. This includes filters, retouching, cropping, overlays, or recompression.

  • Color enhancements or filter usage
  • Beauty retouching and face smoothing
  • Photoshop-like modifications
  • Compression and re-save artifacts

How should users interpret results?

The authenticity score is designed as a decision-support indicator. It should be read along with the explanation, metadata, visual metrics, and file context.

  • Read the score together with explanation
  • Check whether metadata is present or missing
  • Review face detection and metric signals
  • Use source credibility and context before drawing conclusions
